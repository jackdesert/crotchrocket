Description of content of photocd 0340 – 12 March 1996

Translated from Italian to English by Jeremy Reeder, CRL, CPS, June 8-17, 2006.

Numbers in black do not correspond with photograph numbers.  Entries in gray are rough translations by machine, which will soon be replaced with more coherent human translations.  My own comments are in green italic text.  Green numbers refer to photographs. Photographic sequence and description of material confiscated by the MPs from the Giulianova Company on 5 November 1995, near the apartment of Lorenzo Palmeri in Montesilvano (PE)

 

In appendix:

- other material related to the confiscation on 6 June 1995 from Palerino Farino in Rome

- other material relative to the confiscation on 25 November 1995, from the Rinaldi family in Rome

 

1. Mottura lock, complete with two original keys and keyhole cover.  The lock is in good condition and functions properly.

 

2. Close-up of the ornamental keyhole cover.  Some clear marks can be seen that were in all likelyhood left by the tools used by the criminal to determine the code of the plates inside the lock.  Some of these signs are simply from normal use.  The signs most likely left by the tool of the criminal are those semicircular ones, about 3 cm in diameter, that almost surround the two Mottura inscriptions.

 

3. View of both keys given by the bank.  The bank stated that one of these keys was a spare, the other for normal use.

 

4. The normally used key is easily recognizable by the presence of abrasions on the chrome surface and by light wear on the bitting.

 

5. The spare key is clearly identifiable by the almost complete absence of abrasions, caused by use, on the chrome surface.

 

6. Inside of the Mottura lock with cover removed.  The area to look for any signs of picking (action to reconstruct the coding of the lock) is found in the cavity, corresponding to number 6.  It is, in fact, in this zone that the coding of the key does its job.

 

7. (25) The magnified close-up of the coding zone shows clear signs of picking.  These signs are recognizable by the darker color of the concave part and are completely characteristic.

 

8. (21 or 22) More evidence of the use of picks is found in the scratches that appear on the internal surface of the plates. I remind you that these plates normally should not show any sign, because the key in its normal operation does not contact the back and front of the plate.

 

9. (26) The semicircular signs typically left by the end of a pick are visible on this plate also.

 

10. (27) The semicircular signs typically left by the end of a pick are visible on this plate also.

 

11. At their arrest, the criminals were found to possess three double-bitted keys and one key for a cylinder lock.  Tests showed that the cylinder key was for the condominium lock, while the three double-bitted keys were for the three Mottura locks installed on the two bank vault doors.

 

12. A magnified examination of the key to the cylinder lock shows that it was clearly made by hand, with methods of handicraft and not of precision, even if sufficiently precise for the objective of the criminals.

 

13. The blade of the key marked with black tape on its stem shows that the key is clearly handmade.  The criminal who produced it traced by hand the approximate shape of the bitting, which he later reproduced with strokes of a file.

 

14. View of the other side of the blade of the key whose stem has black tape.  On the opposite side, the same track marks are revealed.  Careful examination of the blade shows that it was made by hand filing, since all the threads are perpendicular to the stem, following therefore the forward and backward motion of the file.

 

15. Close-up of the blade of the key marked with black tape, which shows the signs left by the file.

 

16. Blade of the key marked with clear tape, on which are revealed the same track marks and handcraftedness.

 

17. Close-up of the other face of the key that has clear tape on its stem, with similar comments.

 

18. Close-up of the key with white tape on its stem.  In this case, the criminal had to also file down the thickness of the blade.

 

19. Other face of the key with white tape on its stem, in which key, despite the filing of the plated surface, the track marks can still be found.

 

 20. The tool used by the criminal is most likely a sheetmetal marker, known in technical jargon as a “height surface gauge”.  It is a metallic tool with a hard, pointed tip, used to make fine reference marks or perforations on pieces of metal.

 

21. (40 or 43) Series of picks and various tools, confiscated by the military police in Montesilvano on 5 Nov. ’95.  These are shown in detail, in order from left to right.

 

22. Tool that is used to increase hand sensitivity when an attempt is made with the pick to lift every single plate, to find the appropriate coding height.  This is probably the tool that left the semicircular marks found on the lock’s keyhole cover, described already in photo #2.

 

23. The folded brass piece goes into the tool in photo 22; that one is blocked by two screws to prevent the picks from turning it.

 

24. (47) Tool or probe used to find the coding heights.

 

25. (48) Tool probably used to gauge hole depths or plate positions.  I am in no position to describe exactly how this is used.

 

26. Close-up of the tool in photo 25.

 

27. Typical pick for double-bitted locks.  It is made up of two parts: a hollow tube with a tooth which serves to retract the bolt (the so-called tensioner), and a rod with a moveable tooth which serves to raise the plates to the correct height (the so-called feeler).

 

28. Close-up of the pick in photo 27.

 

29. Pick similar to the one described in photo 27, adapted however for locks of another type.

 

30. Close-up of photo 29.

 

31. Another precision tool, used like the tensioner and feeler for double-bitted locks.  The graduated scale, commonly used as a goniometer and applied on the brass keg, allows accurate estimation of the height of the hole in the plate by way of an external repeater.

 

32. Close-up of the tool in photo 31.

 

33. Tool composed of a tensioner and a feeler, adapted for another type of double-bitted lock.

 

34. Close-up of photo 33.

 

35. Here we have a customary tool, but a specially made one to open locks like CISA, in which the bolt is found between the two packs of three plates each.  In this case, the tensioner is found at the center, and the end of the pick must be moved back and forth, as a feeler to feel the three front plates and the three back plates.

 

36. Close-up of the tool in photo 35.

 

37. Block of clay normally used to make imprints of keys.

 

38. Close-up of the block, where the imprint of a key is easily seen.

 

39. Tool of original make, used like a feeler, for cylinder locks.

 

40. Seven marks are found on the bottom of the tool, which correspond to the approximate position of the pins of a cylinder lock.  With this tool, the criminal can find, one by one, the position and height of the pins, with a method that will be better described later.

 

41. Close-up of the tip of the tool.  It must be lodged in the chamber of the pin to be measured; notice that the graded scale is brought back to front of the object as well.

 

42. Here is how this object could be used.  The pick is inserted in the cylinder of the lock, at a depth indicated by the heels, corresponding in this case to one of the five pins to be measured.  The pin is then manually lifted to the maximum possible height.

 

43. Next, the hole produced is measured with a caliper.  This measurement relates to the height of the pins inside the cylinder lock; so it is possible to decode, at least roughly, the coding from the opening.  Minor touch-ups can then be made by hand.

 

44. Series of confiscated keys, marked with the labels Without Code, Luisa, Pav, Invst, F1 F2.

 

45. View of the double-bitted key Without Code.

 

46. Close-up of the key Without Code, in which is found the presence of clay residue.  That makes one suppose that the key was made by hand, using an imprint obtained from someone who got a hold of the genuine keys for just a moment.  The lock that is opened by this key is of the central bolt type, with a pack of four plates before the bolt and another pack of four plates after the bolt.

 

47. Close-up of the other side of the key in figure 45.

 

48. View of the two keys marked with the code Luisa.  One key is cylindrical, of the type commonly called Chicago (properly tubular key), and the other is double-bitted.  It is only partially made, because the criminal coded only the portion of the blade that can be used to operate the lock from the outside, leaving intact the frontal part, which is used only when one wants to operate the lock from the inside and lock himself in the house.  It looks like the lock corresponding to this key must be a Mottura.

 

49. Close-up of the tubular key, which was made, not by hand, with a special milling machine.  I find that they exist in commerce and can be used to craft certain lock picks, in order to open these locks and also to determine the coding.  This type of pick is not, however, present among the material at my disposal.

 

50. Luisa double-bitted key:  close-up.  This key was undoubtedly made by hand, as can be found by the heights of the teeth, which do not match standardized measures.  I must hypothesize, then, that the criminal in this case made an appropriate key by hand.  The file marks and the scrapes that are found in photo 50 and particularly in photo 51 are much like those already observed on the keys in the hands of the robbers.
