How to Directionally Control a Burnout
by Jack Desert



Okay, let's define what we're talking about here.  Burnouts done on a motorcycle, no feet on the ground.  I'm assuming you can already do a burnout, so we'll skip the basics of getting the front tire to stick to the road whilst the rear tire spins madly.  We'll just cover the really good stuff--  how to directionally control that fishtailing bike you're on.

First things first--  spinning the rear tire is what gives the back of the bike some lateral freedom.  When it's not spinning at all, you can apply a large side load to the bike and there is no drift at all.  But when the rear tire is spinning at some velocity relative to the road surface, any side loads the bike experiences will cause some amount of rear wheel drift.  The faster the tire spins, the slicker the tire and road, and the larger the side load on the bike, the greater the drift velocity will be.  

 So, what side loads (forces) are there on the bike?  
  1. Gravity--  the rear always wants to drift downhill.  
  2. Turning forces--  The rear always wants to drift to the outside of the turn.  

Of course, these two can be in the same direction or in opposite directions.

For lotsa' smoke--  spin the tire really fast (I use 3rd gear when possible), make sure you're not on any kind of a sidehill (point the bike uphill if possible, then the pendulum effect works for you), and aim the bike in a straight line by using just enough forward velocity to keep the bike under control.  

For figure 8's and stuff, spin the tire at a much slower rate and use the information in the following table to obtain the drift rate you desire.  


The bike will turn faster when:
  • Entry speed is higher
  • Turn radius is sharper
  • Tire spin speed is higher
  • Drift direction is DOWNhill
  • Tire and road are slippery
  • You slow the bike down during the turn
 The bike will turn slower when:
  • Entry speed is slower
  • Turn radius is shallower
  • Tire pin speed is lower
  • Drift direction is UPhill
  • Tire and road are grippy
  • You let bike speed up during the turn
